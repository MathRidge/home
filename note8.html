Packing Prime Copies with Exponents

When prime pieces repeat, the work can get crowded. Exponents help us pack repeated copies, compare the top shelf with the bottom shelf, and rebuild the simplified fraction without losing focus.

Pack copiesThe exponent counts how many matching prime copies appear.
Compare shelvesUse top count minus bottom count for each prime base.
Place leftoversPositive stays upstairs. Negative moves downstairs. Zero becomes 1.

01 Start with the Crowded Line

We are still using top shelf and bottom shelf thinking. The new goal is to count repeated prime pieces without staring at a long line.

5645 × 54162
Focus checkpoint: do not multiply the big numbers first. Break into prime pieces, then pack the repeated copies.

02 Break First, Then Pack

After factoring, each shelf has a stack of prime copies. Exponents make that stack easier to read.

Top shelf

56 gives 2, 2, 2, 7. Then 54 gives 2, 3, 3, 3.

2227and2333
Bottom shelf

45 gives 3, 3, 5. Then 162 gives 2, 3, 3, 3, 3.

335and23333
The pieces are correct, but the line is busy. This is when exponent packing helps.

03 Exponent Means Copy Count

An exponent is a copy counter. It tells how many times the same prime base appears.

Four copies of 2
2222→24
Three copies of 3
333→33
One copy of 7
7→71
Read 24 as “four copies of 2.”

04 Pack Each Shelf

Now the shelves are clean. Each prime base appears once, and the exponent records the number of copies.

Top shelf packed
24×33×71

Four 2s, three 3s, and one 7 remain listed upstairs.

Bottom shelf packed
21×36×51

One 2, six 3s, and one 5 remain listed downstairs.

Say it: the amount did not change. We only changed crowded copies into cleaner counts.

05 Compare One Base at a Time

Use the same calm comparison for each prime base: top count minus bottom count.

Base 2
top 4−bottom 1→23

Three copies of 2 stay on the top shelf.

Base 3
top 3−bottom 6→3−3

Three copies of 3 stay on the bottom shelf.

A negative exponent is not a new mystery. It means the leftover copies are downstairs.

06 Use the Sign as the Shelf Label

The exponent sign tells where the leftover copies belong.

Positive countleftover copies stay on the top shelf
Negative countleftover copies move to the bottom shelf
Zero countall matching copies cancel into 1

07 Rebuild the Simplified Fraction

After comparison, place the leftovers back onto the correct shelf.

23 × 3−3 × 71 × 5−1
Top leftovers2371
Bottom leftovers3351
2³ × 73³ × 5→56135

08 The Packing Rhythm

Use this short path when the problem starts to feel crowded.

1Factor

Break each number into prime pieces.

2Pack

Count repeated copies with exponents.

3Compare

Top count minus bottom count.

4Place

Positive on top, negative on bottom.

5Finish

Multiply only the leftovers.

Short version: factor → pack → compare → place → finish.
