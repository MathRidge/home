Packing Prime Copies with Exponents

In the previous note, Fraction Multiplication and Division Through Factor Trees, we crossed out matching prime copies one by one. Now we learn a cleaner way to count repeated prime copies.

Same Prime PiecesCollect repeated copies of 2, 3, 5, and 7
Exponent CountThe small number above tells how many copies
Shelf LocationPositive count stays upstairs. Negative count means downstairs.

8.a Start From a Familiar Previous-Note Problem

This problem still uses the same top shelf and bottom shelf idea. We are not learning a brand-new world yet. We are only making the prime pieces easier to read.

5645 × 54162
Main idea: when the prime copies become crowded, we need a cleaner way to count them.

8.b Break Into Prime Copies First

Like the previous note, we break each number apart before multiplying. Then we count the copies of each prime number.

Top Shelf
56 gives 2 2 2 7 and 54 gives 2 3 3 3
Bottom Shelf
45 gives 3 3 5 and 162 gives 2 3 3 3 3
Notice: the copies are correct, but the line is crowded. This is the moment where exponent notation becomes useful.

8.c New Tool: The Exponent Count

When the same prime number repeats, we can pack the repeated copies together. The small number above is the exponent count.

Four copies of 2

2 2 2 2 → 24

Three copies of 3

3 3 3 → 33

One copy of 7

7 → 71
Read 24 as: four copies of 2 are packed together.

8.d Pack the Top Shelf and Bottom Shelf

Now we collect identical prime copies on each shelf.

Top Shelf Packed

24 × 33 × 71

The top shelf has four copies of 2, three copies of 3, and one copy of 7.

Bottom Shelf Packed

21 × 36 × 51

The bottom shelf has one copy of 2, six copies of 3, and one copy of 5.

The fraction is the same. The writing is just cleaner.

8.e Compare Counts Instead of Crossing One at a Time

In the previous note, we crossed out matching copies one at a time. Now we compare the exponent counts.

For the copies of 2

Top has 24, bottom has 21 → 2(4−1) → 23
Parentheses show the action: (4−1). After the action is done, the answer becomes a sign: 23. Three copies of 2 remain on the top shelf.

For the copies of 3

Top has 33, bottom has 36 → 3(3−6) → 3−(6−3) → 3−3
Parentheses show the first action: (3−6). Then we rewrite it as −(6−3): the parentheses still show the count action, and the outside negative sign shows bottom-shelf direction. After the action is done, the answer becomes 3−3. That means three copies of 3 remain on the bottom shelf.

For the copy of 7 and copy of 5

7 with count 1 stays upstairs and 5−1 means one copy of 5 stays downstairs

8.f The Shelf Meaning of the Sign

The exponent sign is a location label.

Positive exponent The prime copies remain on the top shelf.
Negative exponent The prime copies remain on the bottom shelf.
Zero exponent No copies remain. They hide away as 1.
This is the big idea: negative exponent does not need to feel mysterious. It means the leftover copies are downstairs.

8.g Rebuild the Simplified Fraction

From our comparison, we found:

23 × 3−3 × 71 × 5−1
Positive counts go on the top shelf. Negative counts go on the bottom shelf.
23 × 71 33 × 51 → 8 × 7 27 × 5 → 56 135
Final answer: 56/45 × 54/162 reduces to 56/135.

8.h What Does Zero Exponent Mean?

Sometimes the top shelf and bottom shelf have the same count. Then every copy pairs away.

21 21 → 2(1−1) → 20
2 2 → 1
Zero exponent means no matching copy is left to show. The repeated copy is hidden away as a product of 1.

Final Memory

Packing Copies

Repeated prime copies can be packed with an exponent count. For example, four copies of 2 become 24.

Shelf Location

Positive exponent count means top shelf. Negative exponent count means bottom shelf. Zero exponent count means nothing is left except 1.

One big idea: exponents are a cleaner way to count repeated prime copies after using factor trees.
